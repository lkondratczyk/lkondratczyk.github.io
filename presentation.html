 Today's Human Drivers

  • Car accidents kill about 34,000 people every year in the USA, and 1.2 million around the world.
  • There are 2.6 million people injuried in 1.8 million injury accidents in the USA in a typical year, and around 6 million total accidents. There are 50 million injuries worldwide.
  • The NHTSA estimates the total cost to society of accidents at around 230 billion dollars, around 2.5% of the GDP. Expressed per mile driven, it's about 8 cents/mile -- more than the cost of gasoline in a highly efficient car.
  • Studies suggest that 40% of fatal accidents involve drunk driving and that 80% of all accidents are the result of driver inattention.
  • Traffic congestion, caused by more than just accidents, wastes from 4 to 8 billion hours of people's time each year, and results in the burning of 6 billion gallons of extra fuel

The main problem with that is they can potentially have full control of the decision making process. Of course we, as society, cannot allow this to happen.

The Benefits and Implications of Self-Driving Car Networks

Self driving cars offer many tempting advantages.

  • Gated intersections become a thing of the past - computers can guide vehicles between the spaces of others in cross traffic.

  • Computers could effectively manage a smaller distance between cars at higher speeds, increasing freeway capacity and decreasing traffic.

  • These advantages are only fully realized if all cars are self driving, and all are on a common communication network.

Who would be responsible for maintaining such a system?

  • Such a network would have to be centralized in order for all cars to communicate.

  • Having one company be responsible for this means handing them a monopoly on the service.

  • Homeland security poses another obstacle to private ownership of the network.

    • Millions of daily commuters would have their safety depend on the network.

    • The shut-down of transportation systems would be an economic catastrophe.

  • The government would have to be responsible for the network to address these concerns.

The existence of a government-run transportation network has serious implications.

  • The Supreme Court has already unanimously ruled against location-based tracking without a warrant.
    • Supreme Court Ruling on GPS Tracking
      • "...reasonable expectations of privacy [are] violated by the long-term monitoring of the movements of [vehicles]."
      "...five justices suggested in concurring statements that a warrant might similarly be needed for prolonged surveillance through smartphones or other devices with GPS capabilities."

  • We can't be on an interconnected network without signing away our right have our location known.

  • Would the government and law enforcement take advantage of this and keep track of our exact location and habits?

They've already done everything they can to get around this law.

  • License plate scanning technology has been around for a long time.

  • Law enforcement has combined this technology with surveillance systems to create dragnet tracking and massive databases of people's movements.
    • ACLU article on how local law enforcement currently employs this technology.
      • "In our society, it is a core principle that the government does not invade people’s privacy and collect information about citizens’ innocent activities just in case they do something wrong."
    • Washington Post article on Federal government plans to centralize the process.
      • "...the database could easily contain more than 1 billion records and could be shared with other law enforcement agencies, raising concerns that the movements of ordinary citizens who are under no criminal suspicion could be scrutinized."

  • This type of tracking is very limited - it requires massive storage to maintain, and doesn't give accurate real-time location of individuals.

What Self-Driving Cars Means for...

Insurance

  • The need for insurance will decrease because as we move into self-driving cars the liability shifts from driver to manufacturer of the vehicle, the software developer, and any third parties related.

  • “If nondiscretionary-communicative vehicles became mandatory (as the introduction of either the interactive or remote-controlled models might require), there would be no need for private insurance, since all liability for AVs would be transferred to manufacturers”
